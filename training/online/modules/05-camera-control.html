Module 4

Camera Control

⏱️ 45 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Switch between wide, zoom, and thermal cameras
  • Understand thermal palettes and when to use each
  • Use the laser rangefinder to measure distances
  • Enable Night Scene Mode for low-light operations
  • Use the NIR spotlight on the M4T for invisible illumination
  • Capture 360° panoramas for area documentation

4.1 Camera Types

DJI enterprise drones supported by Eagle Eyes carry multiple cameras on the same gimbal. You can switch between them at any time during flight using the camera selector buttons on the right side of the Fly Screen (labeled W, T, Z, ZT).

CameraButtonWhat It Does
WideWThe main RGB camera with the widest field of view. Best for general search and area coverage. This is where AI detection runs.
ZoomZOptical zoom camera for investigating detections up close. Use when you spot something and need a closer look without descending the drone.
ThermalTInfrared thermal camera that shows heat signatures. Available on M3T, M30T, M4T, and H30T drones only. See Module 3 for thermal detection details.
Zoom + ThermalZTSplit-screen mode showing zoom and thermal side by side. Useful for cross-referencing a heat signature with the visual image simultaneously.
💡
AI detection only runs on the Wide (W) camera feed. When you switch to Zoom, Thermal, or split-screen, the AI detector pauses. Switch back to Wide to resume automated detection.

4.2 Thermal Palettes

When viewing the thermal camera feed, you can change the color palette to control how temperatures are displayed. Different palettes make different features easier to see depending on the conditions.

PaletteAppearanceWhen to Use
White HotHotter objects appear brighter/whiter, cooler objects darkerGeneral purpose — the most commonly used palette. Good in most conditions.
Black HotHotter objects appear darker/blacker, cooler objects lighterUseful in bright daytime conditions where a bright screen is hard to read. Some operators find dark targets easier to spot.
TintWarm color tint overlay on the thermal imageAdds a subtle color wash that can make thermal features easier to distinguish while retaining a natural feel. Good alternative to White Hot when you want a bit more visual contrast.
Iron RedDark purple/blue for cold, through red/orange to bright yellow/white for hotFamiliar "heat vision" display. Good for presentations and reports as it intuitively communicates temperature.
💡
Start with White Hot for most SAR operations. Switch to Iron Red if you want an intuitive hot-to-cold color display, or Tint for subtle color contrast.

4.3 Laser Rangefinder

Enterprise DJI drones equipped with a laser rangefinder can measure the distance from the drone to any point the camera is aimed at. This is useful for estimating how far a detection is from the operator, measuring distances between landmarks, or documenting scene dimensions.

DroneRange
M30 / M30T3m – 1,500m (1.5 km)
M4T3m – 3,000m (3 km)
H30T3m – 3,000m (3 km)

How to Measure

  1. Point the gimbal at the target you want to measure
  2. Tap the Laser button on the Fly Screen
  3. The measured distance appears on screen
  4. The GPS coordinates of the laser point are calculated and placed on the map as a ⊕ Laser Point marker

Laser points persist on the map for the duration of the session and can be used as reference markers for ground teams.

Laser Rangefinder in Eagle Eyes Pilot

In the Eagle Eyes app, the laser rangefinder is accessible through the Quick Status & Actions dialog. Look for the "Range Finder" card, which displays:

  • On: 45.2m — when the laser is active and returning a measurement
  • Off — when the laser is inactive

Short-tap the card to see the current status. Long-tap to toggle the laser on or off. When a measurement is taken, the laser point coordinates are plotted on the map with a red pin marker (📍). You can also use laser location coordinates when capturing markers — the Capture Options dialog lets you choose between Drone Location and Laser Location for placing map markers at the measured point instead of the drone's position.

4.4 Night Scene Mode

Night Scene Mode is a camera enhancement that improves visibility in low-light conditions. When enabled, the drone's zoom camera applies advanced image processing to brighten and clarify the feed, making it possible to see details that would otherwise be invisible in darkness.

How It Works

  • Night Scene Mode operates on the Zoom (Z) camera lens
  • The camera uses longer exposure times and noise reduction algorithms to produce a brighter, cleaner image
  • The effect is most noticeable in twilight, dusk, dawn, and nighttime conditions

Night Scene Options

SettingBehavior
AutoThe drone automatically enables night scene processing when it detects low ambient light. Recommended for most operations.
EnableForces night scene processing on regardless of light level. Use when you want consistent low-light enhancement.
DisableTurns off night scene processing entirely. Use in daylight or when you need unprocessed imagery.

Accessing Night Scene in Eagle Eyes

Night Scene Mode is available in the Camera Settings dialog. Open it from the camera settings controls on the Fly Screen and look for the Night Scene Mode dropdown.

💡
Leave Night Scene set to Auto for most SAR operations. The drone will enable it when needed and disable it in good light, so you don't have to manage it manually.

4.5 NIR Spotlight (M4T Only)

The NIR (Near-Infrared) spotlight is an invisible light source built into the M4T that illuminates a scene for the zoom camera without producing visible light. This is valuable for nighttime SAR where you need to see detail without alerting or blinding a subject, or disturbing wildlife.

How NIR Works

  • The NIR spotlight emits near-infrared light at a wavelength invisible to the human eye
  • The zoom camera sensor can detect this reflected light, producing a visible image on your screen
  • Effective illumination range is approximately 100 meters
  • Requires Night Scene Mode to be Enabled on the drone
  • Requires 7x zoom or higher on the zoom camera

Requirements

RequirementDetail
DroneM4T only — other drones do not have a built-in NIR emitter
Night SceneMust be set to Enable (not Auto or Disable)
Zoom Level7x or higher on the Zoom camera
Range~100m effective illumination

Using NIR in Eagle Eyes

In the Quick Status & Actions dialog, look for the card labeled "NIR Light (M4T, 🔍≥7x)" with a 💡 icon. Short-tap to see the requirements. Long-tap to toggle the NIR light on or off.

⚠️
If the NIR light fails to enable, check that you are on an M4T, that Night Scene Mode is set to Enable (not Auto), and that your zoom is at 7x or higher.

4.6 360° Panorama New in 1.6.0

Eagle Eyes can capture ultra high-resolution 360° panoramas by automatically rotating the drone and stitching multiple images together. This produces a comprehensive view of the area for documentation, briefings, or post-mission analysis.

Panorama Zoom Modes

ModeDetail LevelCapture TimeBest For
StandardCurrent zoom levelFastestGeneral area documentation
2X ZoomHigher detailModerateBalance of speed and detail
7X ZoomMaximum detailLongestDetailed scene documentation where you need to zoom into areas later

Capturing a Panorama

  1. Fly to desired altitude (60-100m typical for a good overview)
  2. Ensure the drone is in a stable hover
  3. Tap ☰ Menu → Capture → 360° Panorama
  4. Select your zoom mode (Standard, 2X, or 7X)
  5. Tap Start Capture
  6. Wait for the automated sequence to complete — the drone will rotate itself
⚠️
Do not touch the flight controls during panorama capture. The automated gimbal/yaw sequence needs to complete without interruption. If interrupted, the panorama will be incomplete.

Thermal Panoramas

On thermal-equipped drones (M3T, M30T, M4T, H30T), you can capture thermal panoramas by switching to the Thermal camera before starting the capture. This produces a 360° thermal overview that is excellent for identifying heat sources spread across a wide area.

When to use panoramas: Documenting a search area before/after sweeps, creating reference imagery for ground teams, recording incident scenes for reports.

When NOT to use: During active subject search (use live detection instead), time-critical operations, or when subjects may be moving.
